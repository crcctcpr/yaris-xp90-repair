# Cooling System Guide — 2011 Toyota Yaris XP90

---

## 1. THERMOSTAT LOCATION AND REPLACEMENT

### Thermostat Specification
- **Type:** 82°C opening temperature (1KR-FE / 2NZ-FE standard)
- **Housing location (1KR-FE):** Bottom hose side of engine — the thermostat is mounted in a housing on the lower-left of the cylinder head where the bottom radiator hose connects
- **Housing location (2NZ-FE):** Similar, bottom hose side

### Symptoms of Failed Thermostat
- Engine runs cold (temperature gauge stays below normal operating range)
- Engine runs hot (temperature gauge climbs and car overheats)
- Erratic temperature gauge behavior (fluctuates)
- Heater does not produce hot air (thermostat stuck closed)
- Engine takes a very long time to reach operating temperature

### Tools Required
- Drain pan (for coolant)
- Socket set (10mm, 12mm)
- Gasket scraper or plastic card (not metal — don't scratch aluminum)
- Torque wrench
- New thermostat and gasket/O-ring

### Procedure

1. **Safety:** Allow engine to cool completely — coolant and the thermostat housing will be hot.
2. Disconnect battery negative terminal.
3. Remove the plastic engine cover (2–3 clips).
4. Place drain pan under the bottom hose connection.
5. Loosen the hose clamp on the bottom radiator hose (10mm hose clamp nut) — do NOT remove the hose yet.
6. Remove the thermostat housing bolts (typically 2–3 bolts, 10mm or 12mm) securing the housing to the engine head.
7. Lift the thermostat housing off — coolant will spill from the hose connection point. Allow to drain into the pan.
8. The thermostat is now visible inside the housing — it is a cylindrical metal cartridge with a spring end (cold end) and a valve end (hot end).
9. Withdraw the thermostat — note the orientation (the jiggle pin should point upward).
10. Inspect the gasket/O-ring — if damaged or flattened: use a new one.
11. Clean the mating surface inside the housing with a gasket scraper and brake cleaner.
12. Install the new 82°C thermostat — ensure the jiggle pin points upward (toward the engine block).
13. Install new gasket/O-ring around the thermostat (or on the housing surface if the kit includes a full gasket).
14. Reinstall the housing and torque bolts to **20 Nm**.
15. Reconnect the bottom hose — tighten the hose clamp securely (clamp nut approximately 8 Nm).
16. Reconnect battery and fill coolant reservoir to MAX.
17. Start the engine — the cooling fan should activate when the coolant reaches ~85°C. Listen for any leaks.
18. After the engine has run for 5 minutes, let it cool and recheck coolant level — top up if needed.

**Thermostat Part:** Toyota Part: 16110-40020 (1KR-FE, 82°C) — OEM $20–$40; Aftermarket $10–$25

## 2. WATER PUMP REPLACEMENT

### Water Pump Type & Location
- **1KR-FE:** Timing chain driven — located on the front/right side of the engine, driven directly by the timing chain
- **2NZ-FE:** Timing belt driven — must be replaced whenever the timing belt is replaced (every 100,000 km)

### Symptoms of Failed Water Pump
- Coolant leaking from the pump weep hole (small hole on the side/bottom of the pump body)
- Engine overheating (pump not circulating coolant)
- Grinding or squealing noise from the pump area
- Coolant in the engine oil (pump seal failure, rare but severe)

### Procedure (1KR-FE)

1. Allow engine to cool completely.
2. Drain the coolant (see section 3 for coolant flush procedure).
3. Remove the accessory drive belt (tensioner pulley, 14mm bolt).
4. Remove the alternator (2 bolts, disconnect wiring).
5. Remove the crank pulley (impact wrench, very tight).
6. Remove the timing cover (front plastic cover, 10mm bolts).
7. The water pump is visible behind the timing cover, on the timing chain side of the engine.
8. Remove the water pump bolts (typically 3–4 bolts, 10mm).
9. Withdraw the old water pump — have a drain pan ready for residual coolant.
10. Clean the pump mounting surface on the engine block with brake cleaner and gasket scraper.
11. If the new pump comes with a gasket: install it (use gasket sealer if specified).
12. If pump uses an O-ring: apply a light coat of coolant to the O-ring before installation.
13. Install the new pump and torque bolts to **10–12 Nm** (check Toyota spec — typically low torque).
14. Reconnect timing cover bolts.
15. Reinstall crank pulley, alternator, drive belt.
16. Refill coolant (see section 3).

**Water Pump Part (1KR-FE):** Toyota Part: 16100-40020 — OEM $40–$80; Aftermarket $20–$50

### Procedure (2NZ-FE)
- Water pump replacement on the 2NZ-FE is performed during timing belt replacement.
- See ENGINE_REPAIR.md for the complete timing belt replacement procedure, which includes water pump removal and installation.

## 3. RADIATOR REPLACEMENT

### Symptoms of Radiator Failure
- Coolant leaking from the radiator (visible bright green fluid under the car)
- Engine overheating even with a functioning thermostat and water pump
- Radiator core is clogged (visually blocked fins)
- Previous collision damage (bent fins, crumpled tank)

### Procedure

1. Allow engine to cool.
2. Place drain pan under the radiator.
3. Locate the radiator drain cock at the bottom of the radiator (left or right side, small valve) and open it — coolant will drain.
4. Disconnect the upper and lower radiator hoses (hose clamp nuts, 10mm) — allow remaining coolant to drain into the pan.
5. Disconnect any transmission cooler lines (if equipped — small lines connecting to the bottom of the radiator) using a wrench.
6. Disconnect the electric cooling fan connector (push-tab).
7. Remove the radiator mounting brackets (typically 2 bolts, one on each side, 10mm) — note how they are oriented.
8. Carefully lift the radiator straight up out of the engine bay — avoid catching the radiator on the AC condenser or other components.
9. Position the new radiator in the same orientation as the old one.
10. Install mounting bolts and torque to approximately 8 Nm (snug, not tight — radiators are aluminum and can crack if over-tightened).
11. Reconnect the electric cooling fan connector.
12. Reconnect transmission cooler lines (if equipped) — torque to 10 Nm.
13. Reconnect the upper and lower radiator hoses — position clamps away from the radiator end tanks (to prevent pinching the hose).
14. Tighten hose clamps (approximately 8 Nm).
15. Close the radiator drain cock.
16. Refill the coolant system and bleed air (see section 4).

**Radiator Part:** Toyota Part: 16400-XX100 (varies by model/market) — OEM $100–$250; Aftermarket $50–$150

## 4. COOLANT FLUSH & AIR BLEEDING

### Coolant Specification
- **Type:** Toyota SLLC (Super Long Life Coolant) — mix 50/50 with distilled water
- **Color:** Bright green (different from older red long-life coolants)
- **Capacity:** Approximately 4.0–4.5 liters (varies by engine — check manual)
- **Do NOT mix different coolant types** — different inhibitor packages can react and cause corrosion

### When to Flush
- Every 2 years or 40,000 km
- If coolant is brown/muddy (indicates rust/contamination)
- After any cooling system work (thermostat, water pump, radiator replacement)

### Flush Procedure

1. **Drain the system:**
   - Allow engine to cool
   - Open the radiator drain cock and let the coolant drain into a pan
   - Loosen the lower radiator hose at the radiator and drain remaining coolant
   - If the engine has a block drain plug (some Yaris models do), locate and open it as well — additional coolant will drain

2. **Flush with water:**
   - Close the drain cock
   - Reconnect radiator hose
   - Fill the radiator to MAX with distilled water (tap water can introduce minerals, but distilled is preferred)
   - Start the engine and run at idle for 3–5 minutes — the water will circulate through the system
   - Shut off engine and let cool for 10 minutes
   - Drain the radiator again (open drain cock and loosen hoses)
   - Repeat the water fill and drain cycle 2–3 times until the drained water runs clear

3. **Refill with coolant:**
   - Close all drain cocks and reconnect hoses
   - Fill the radiator with 50/50 pre-mixed Toyota SLLC coolant (to the MAX line)
   - Fill the overflow/expansion reservoir to the MIN or marked "Cold" line

4. **Bleed air from the system:**
   - Start the engine and let it warm to normal operating temperature
   - You will hear the thermostat open (~82°C) — at that point, coolant will flow rapidly through the system
   - Turn on the heater to maximum hot setting — this allows heater core passages to bleed
   - Continue running until the cooling fan activates
   - Shut off engine and let cool for 10 minutes
   - Recheck coolant level in the radiator and reservoir — add more coolant if needed
   - **Repeat the warm-up cycle** to ensure no air remains trapped

### Air Bleeding Location
If the vehicle has a coolant bleeder screw (small valve at the top of the thermostat housing or radiator neck), open it slightly while filling the coolant to allow trapped air to escape, then close it before running the engine.

## 5. ELECTRIC COOLING FAN — RELAY TEST & TEMPERATURE SENSOR

### Symptoms of Fan Failure
- Engine overheats but the cooling fan does not activate
- Cooling fan runs constantly (sensor stuck at "hot")
- Fan makes grinding or rattling noise (bearing failure)
- Fan does not respond to AC being turned on

### Cooling Fan Components
1. **Electric motor:** Draws 10–20A depending on speed
2. **Thermostat switch or ECM-controlled relay:** Activates the fan when coolant reaches ~85°C
3. **Temperature sensor:** Sends coolant temperature signal to the ECM

### Fan Operation
- The cooling fan is typically controlled by the ECM based on coolant temperature sensor input
- When coolant temperature reaches approximately 85°C: ECM signals the fan relay to energize
- When coolant drops below 80°C: ECM de-energizes the relay and the fan stops
- If AC is turned on: the ECM may activate the fan immediately regardless of temperature (to help the AC condenser)

### Relay Test Procedure

1. Locate the cooling fan relay in the engine bay fuse box (consult the fuse box diagram).
2. With the engine cool, remove the relay by pulling it straight out of the socket.
3. Using a multimeter set to resistance (ohms):
   - Measure across the relay's coil pins (typically pins 1 and 3 or 2 and 4) — should read 60–100 Ω when at room temperature
   - If open circuit (infinite resistance): relay coil failed — replace relay
4. Test the relay contacts:
   - With a 12V power source (battery), apply voltage to the relay's trigger pins — you should hear a click as the internal switch closes
   - Measure continuity across the normally-open contact pins — should be open (infinite Ω) when not powered, and near 0 Ω when powered
   - If no continuity when powered: relay contacts failed — replace relay

**Cooling Fan Relay Part:** Toyota Part: 90987-02002 (varies) — $10–$30

### Temperature Sensor Test
1. Locate the coolant temperature sensor (mounted on the thermostat housing or engine head — has a connector and a single wire).
2. With the engine cold (below 20°C):
   - Disconnect the sensor
   - Measure resistance across the sensor — should be approximately 2.5 kΩ at 20°C
3. With the engine warm (above 85°C):
   - Allow the engine to reach operating temperature
   - Measure resistance again — should drop to approximately 0.3–0.5 kΩ at 85–90°C
4. If resistance does not change significantly: sensor is failed — replace it

**Temperature Sensor Part:** Toyota Part: 90919-05040 (varies) — $15–$50

### Direct Fan Test (12V DC)
1. Disconnect the fan motor connector.
2. Using a 12V battery and jumper wires, apply 12V directly to the motor pins:
   - One wire to the + pin, one to the – (ground) pin
   - The fan should spin if the motor is functional
3. Reverse the polarity — the fan should spin in the opposite direction (this tests both motor connections)
4. If the fan does not spin: motor bearing is seized or windings are burnt — replace the fan motor assembly

## 6. HEAD GASKET SYMPTOMS & DIAGNOSIS

### Symptoms of Failed Head Gasket
- **Coolant in the oil:** Oil dipstick shows milky/foamy appearance or "coffee" color
- **Exhaust gas in the coolant:** Bubbles visible in the coolant reservoir while engine is running
- **Overheating:** Engine temperature climbs despite a functioning thermostat and water pump
- **Misfire / rough running:** Combustion gases leaking into a cylinder's cooling jacket
- **Loss of coolant:** Coolant level drops without any visible external leaks
- **White smoke from exhaust:** Steam from coolant entering the combustion chamber

### Diagnosis Procedures

#### Visual Inspection
1. Remove the radiator cap while the engine is warm (not hot — 60–70°C).
2. Have someone start the engine while you observe the radiator neck.
3. If you see air bubbles rising in the coolant for more than 5 seconds: indicates exhaust gas bubbling into the coolant (classic head gasket sign).

#### Combustion Gas Test (Most Definitive)
1. Obtain a combustion gas detector kit (or use a combustion gas analyzer).
2. With the engine running at idle, place the detector hose into the coolant reservoir opening.
3. If combustion gases are detected: head gasket is leaking between a combustion chamber and the coolant jacket.
4. This test is nearly 100% diagnostic for head gasket failure.

#### Compression Test with Coolant Leak Test
1. Perform a standard compression test (see ENGINE_REPAIR.md, section 6).
2. If compression is low on all cylinders uniformly: worn rings or piston issue (not head gasket).
3. If compression is low on 2 adjacent cylinders (e.g., #1 and #2): likely head gasket between those cylinders.
4. After taking compression readings, remove the spark plugs and pour approximately 1 teaspoon of oil into each plug hole.
5. Perform compression test again (wet compression test):
   - If wet compression rises significantly: worn rings
   - If wet compression remains low: burned valve or head damage

#### Pressure Test
1. A cooling system pressure tester (12 psi pump) can be used on the radiator cap opening.
2. If the system loses pressure immediately: external leak or head gasket failure.
3. A slow pressure drop over 1–2 minutes may indicate an internal gasket leak.

### Head Gasket Replacement
Replacement requires:
- Engine removal (full engine teardown) or cylinder head removal (easier but still complex)
- Professional machine shop work to verify the head is not warped
- Specialized tools (head bolt torque sequence, cylinder head gasket, OEM hardware)

**Professional labor cost:** $500–$1500+ (highly variable by location and engine condition)

## 7. OVERHEATING — STEP-BY-STEP DIAGNOSIS FLOWCHART

```
ENGINE OVERHEATING (Temperature gauge in red, warning light on)?

1. CHECK COOLANT LEVEL
   └─ Level LOW?
      ├─ YES: Is there visible coolant under the car?
      │  ├─ Leak at hose: Clamp it or replace hose
      │  ├─ Leak at radiator: Replace radiator
      │  ├─ Leak at pump: Replace water pump
      │  └─ Leak at thermostat: Replace thermostat housing gasket
      └─ NO: Go to step 2

2. CHECK COOLING FAN OPERATION
   └─ Is the electric cooling fan spinning when the car overheats?
      ├─ NO: Fan not running
      │  ├─ Check fan relay (see section 5)
      │  ├─ Check temperature sensor (see section 5)
      │  ├─ Test fan motor with 12V (see section 5)
      │  └─ Replace failed component
      └─ YES: Fan is running. Go to step 3

3. CHECK FOR AIRFLOW BLOCKAGE
   └─ Is the radiator core or AC condenser clogged with debris/dead bugs?
      ├─ YES: Clean radiator fins with a soft brush or compressed air
      └─ NO: Go to step 4

4. CHECK THERMOSTAT
   └─ Does the engine take an unusually long time to reach operating temperature?
      ├─ YES: Thermostat likely stuck open (not heating)
      │  └─ Replace thermostat (see section 1)
      └─ NO: Go to step 5

5. CHECK WATER PUMP
   └─ Is the water pump circulating coolant?
      ├─ Check: With engine running, feel the upper and lower radiator hoses
      │  ├─ Upper hose is cool, lower hose is hot: Water pump may not be pumping
      │  │  └─ Replace water pump (see section 2)
      │  └─ Both hoses are hot: Water pump is circulating
      └─ Go to step 6

6. CHECK FOR HEAD GASKET FAILURE
   └─ Are there air bubbles in the coolant (see section 6)?
      ├─ YES: Perform combustion gas test (see section 6)
      │  └─ If positive: Head gasket has failed
      │     └─ Engine requires major repair (see section 6)
      └─ NO: Go to step 7

7. CHECK BELT TENSION & PULLEYS
   └─ If equipped with a belt-driven water pump:
      ├─ Inspect the accessory drive belt for cracks or fraying
      ├─ Check belt tension (should require moderate finger pressure at the midpoint)
      └─ Replace belt if worn or tensioner if pulley is damaged

8. LAST RESORT: INTERNAL RESTRICTION
   └─ If all above checks pass but overheating persists:
      ├─ Engine cooling passages may be heavily scaled (mineral deposits)
      ├─ Perform a cooling system flush and fill with descaling coolant
      ├─ Or: Take to a machine shop for block testing and flushing
      └─ If a cylinder head was recently installed, there may be casting sand trapped inside the passages
```
