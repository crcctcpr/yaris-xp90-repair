# Brake Guide — 2011 Toyota Yaris XP90

---

## 1. FRONT DISC BRAKE PAD REPLACEMENT

### Specifications
- **Caliper type:** Single-piston sliding caliper
- **Pad material:** Ceramic or semi-metallic (OEM Toyota uses ceramic)
- **Minimum pad thickness:** 1.0 mm (should replace at 2.0 mm for safety and noise prevention)
- **Rotor diameter (OD):** 255 mm
- **Rotor minimum thickness:** 17.0 mm
- **Pad set for both fronts:** Part number 04465-XX024 (varies by market)

### Tools Required
- 14mm wrench or socket (slide pin bolts)
- C-clamp or brake pad spreader
- Torque wrench
- Brake cleaner and wire brush
- Drain pan for brake fluid

### Symptoms of Worn Pads
- Brake warning light on (low brake fluid from pad wear)
- Squealing noise when braking
- Reduced braking power or longer stopping distance
- Soft brake pedal

### Procedure

1. **Safety first:** Park on level ground, set parking brake, turn off engine.
2. Loosen wheel lug nuts slightly (vehicle on ground).
3. Raise front of vehicle and support on jack stands.
4. Remove wheel.
5. **Inspect pads:** Look through the caliper to see pad thickness. If below 2.0 mm: replacement needed.

#### Pad Removal
1. Locate the 2 caliper slide pin bolts on the back of the caliper (bottom and top, 14mm).
2. Loosen and remove both slide pin bolts using a wrench (hold the bolt from the back, wrench on the nut side).
3. The caliper will now swing upward (it is attached to the rotor by the inner pad only).
4. Withdraw the old brake pads from the caliper and rotor — they may require gentle prying with a screwdriver.
5. Inspect the caliper piston for leakage (seeping brake fluid) — if leaking: caliper rebuild/replacement needed.

#### Preparation
1. Using a C-clamp or brake pad spreader, push the caliper piston back into its bore — this makes room for the thicker new pads.
2. Clean the rotor surface and caliper mounting surfaces with brake cleaner and a wire brush — remove any rust or contamination.
3. Inspect the rotor for warping or scoring (see section 2 for rotor replacement criteria).

#### Pad Installation
1. Install the new inner pad (thicker shim side) into the caliper piston recess — it should seat flat against the piston.
2. Install the new outer pad into the caliper mounting bracket — it should slide into the slot.
3. Clean the slide pins with brake cleaner and inspect for rust or corrosion. Apply a thin coat of brake grease (lithium or silicone) to the pins.
4. Lower the caliper down over the pads and rotor.
5. Reinstall the 2 slide pin bolts and torque to **29 Nm** (do not over-tighten — can crack the caliper).
6. Repeat for the other front wheel.

#### Final Steps
1. Pump the brake pedal 5–6 times with the car still raised to seat the pads against the rotor and bring the brake fluid back to the correct level.
2. Check brake fluid level in the master cylinder reservoir — top up with DOT 3 or DOT 4 fluid if needed (do not overfill).
3. Reinstall wheels and torque lug nuts to **103 Nm**.
4. Lower vehicle and perform a test drive in an empty parking lot — apply steady pressure to verify brake function (pads need break-in).

## 2. FRONT ROTOR REPLACEMENT

### When to Replace
- Scored or heavily worn (use a straightedge to check for grooves — depth >0.5mm = replace)
- Warped (brake pedal pulsates when braking) — minimum parallelism is 0.05mm total runout
- Minimum thickness reached: 17.0 mm
- Rusted or cracked

### Procedure

1. Remove the front wheel (following step 1–4 from section 1 above).
2. Remove the brake caliper assembly (2 slide pin bolts, 14mm) — do NOT let it hang by the brake line. Support it with a wire hook or bungee to the frame.
3. Remove the brake pads (see section 1).
4. The rotor is now exposed. It typically pushes/pulls directly off the hub.
5. If rotor is stuck (seized):
   - Spray penetrant oil (WD-40, Liquid Wrench) around the rotor bore
   - Wait 10 minutes
   - Rock the rotor gently back and forth
   - Use a rubber mallet to tap the rotor loose around its circumference
6. Once loose, pull the rotor straight toward you. It should slide off the hub.

#### Rotor Installation
1. Clean the hub surface thoroughly — remove any rust or debris with a wire brush.
2. Slide new rotor onto hub — it should fit snugly.
3. If rotor will not slide onto hub: inspect hub spline for corrosion or damage. Use a file to smooth any rough spots.
4. Once rotor is installed, reinstall brake pads and caliper (see section 1).
5. Do NOT drive the vehicle until brake work is complete — calipers must be secured.

#### Break-In
1. After pad and rotor replacement, the pads and rotor need break-in (conditioning).
2. Drive conservatively for the first 100 km.
3. Avoid heavy braking for the first 20–30 minutes of driving.
4. Perform gentle, gradual stops to allow the pad material to layer onto the rotor surface.

**Front Rotor Part:** Toyota Part: 43512-XX180 (varies by market) — OEM $40–$80 per rotor; Aftermarket $20–$50

## 3. REAR DRUM BRAKE SERVICE

### Specifications
- **Drum internal diameter (ID):** 200 mm
- **Maximum drum ID:** 201.0 mm (if exceeded: replace drum)
- **Brake shoe lining minimum thickness:** 1.0 mm
- **Drum type:** Leading/trailing drum brakes (non-servo design common on small economy cars)

### Symptoms of Worn Rear Brakes
- Brake pedal soft or spongy (air in lines or worn shoes)
- Loud grinding noise when reversing or braking (shoe lining worn through to metal)
- Brake fluid low (wheel cylinder leaks)
- Parking brake ineffective

### Tools Required
- Drum brake removal tool (or three 8mm bolts to use as leverage)
- Wheel cylinder removal/rebuild tools
- Brake spring pliers
- Torque wrench
- Measuring caliper or drum micrometer (to check drum ID)

### Drum Removal Procedure

1. Raise and support rear vehicle on jack stands.
2. Remove wheel.
3. Release the parking brake fully.
4. The drum may be stuck to the hub. **Do NOT pry directly on the drum flange** — use a drum puller tool or install 3 bolts (8mm) into the puller holes on the back of the drum and turn them equally to draw the drum off the hub. These puller holes are 120° apart.
5. Once the drum is free, pull it straight toward you off the hub.

#### Drum Inspection
1. Measure the internal diameter with a caliper or drum micrometer.
2. If ID is less than 200.5 mm: drum can be reused.
3. If ID exceeds 201.0 mm: drum must be replaced.
4. If drum has cracks: do not reuse — replace.
5. If drum is scored but under max ID: can be turned (machined) at a machine shop or brake shop (~$10–$20 per drum).

### Brake Shoe Inspection & Replacement

#### Inspection
1. With the drum removed, visually inspect the brake shoes and linings.
2. Measure the remaining lining thickness — if below 1.0 mm: replace shoes.
3. Check for oil or brake fluid contamination on the linings — indicates a wheel cylinder leak.
4. If wheel cylinder is leaking: it must be replaced or rebuilt before new shoes are installed (new shoes will be fouled by the leak).

#### Shoe Removal (if replacement is needed)
1. Locate the brake shoe springs — they are held in place by spring pins and clips on the backing plate.
2. Using brake spring pliers, compress the springs and rotate the pins to release them.
3. Gently remove the brake shoes — they are connected by a self-adjuster mechanism and parking brake cable.
4. Disconnect the parking brake cable from the shoes and the self-adjuster link.
5. Remove the old shoes completely.

#### Shoe Installation
1. Clean all rust and contamination from the backing plate with a wire brush.
2. Apply a light coat of brake grease to the backing plate contact points (where the shoes slide).
3. Install the new brake shoes — align the self-adjuster and parking brake cable linkage correctly.
4. Reinstall the spring pins and clips — compress springs with pliers as before.
5. The shoes should move freely on the backing plate but should not rattle excessively.

### Wheel Cylinder Service (if leaking)

#### Symptoms
- Brake fluid seeping from behind the drum
- Brake shoe lining soaked with fluid
- Soft brake pedal (internal piston seal failure)

#### Rebuild vs. Replace
- If cylinder is leaking: can be rebuilt (reseal) for ~$15–$25 labor + seal kit ($5–$10), or replaced with a remanufactured unit ($25–$50).

#### Removal
1. Disconnect the brake line from the wheel cylinder (10mm nut, have a small catch pan ready).
2. Remove 2 mounting bolts (10mm) securing the cylinder to the backing plate.
3. Remove the cylinder — brake fluid will drip.

#### Installation (New or Rebuilt Cylinder)
1. Install new/rebuilt wheel cylinder and torque bolts to **11 Nm**.
2. Reconnect brake line and torque to **11 Nm**.
3. Install new brake shoes (see above).
4. Reinstall drum and wheel.
5. Bleed brake system (see section 6).

**Wheel Cylinder Part:** OEM $20–$50; Remanufactured $15–$40

### Drum Reinstallation & Adjustment
1. Before installing the drum, perform final inspection of shoes and backing plate.
2. Install the drum by reversing removal procedure.
3. The brake shoes will need to be adjusted to the drum diameter — modern vehicles have automatic adjusters that operate in reverse. Drive in reverse a few times at low speed while braking gently — the shoes will self-adjust.
4. Reinstall wheel and torque lug nuts to **103 Nm**.

## 4. BRAKE CALIPER REBUILD / REPLACEMENT

### When Needed
- Caliper piston sticks / does not retract (causes uneven braking, pad drag)
- Brake fluid seeps from caliper seal
- Caliper piston is corroded or pitted
- Caliper bore is damaged

### Rebuild vs. Replace
- **Rebuild:** Disassemble caliper, replace internal seals and piston — cost $30–$80 labor + kit.
- **Replace:** Install remanufactured caliper unit — cost $40–$120 per caliper, includes rebuild and testing.

### Caliper Rebuild Procedure (DIY possible but requires precision work)

1. Remove caliper from vehicle (see section 1, steps 1–5).
2. Drain brake fluid from caliper — loosen bleeder screw at the top of caliper.
3. Using a C-clamp, slowly push the piston out of the bore — monitor the piston progress. If stuck: soak the caliper in penetrant oil.
4. Remove the dust seal (rubber ring around piston).
5. Using a small plastic or brass rod, gently tap the piston out the back side of the bore if it is stuck.
6. Remove the inner seal (rubber ring in the bore, deeper than dust seal).
7. Clean the bore thoroughly with brake cleaner and a soft brass brush — do not scratch the polished bore surface (scratches cause seal failure).
8. Obtain a caliper rebuild kit (includes new piston seal, dust seal, bleeder screw cap).
9. Install new inner seal into the bore groove using a seal installer tool (or carefully by hand with a plastic rod).
10. Coat the piston with brake fluid and install the new dust seal.
11. Carefully insert the piston into the bore, keeping it straight — it should slide smoothly.
12. Install the dust seal retainer (if equipped).
13. Reinstall the caliper onto the vehicle and bleed the system.

**Rebuild Kit:** $15–$30 per caliper

### Caliper Replacement
- Purchase a remanufactured caliper (tested and sealed).
- Remove old caliper, install new one, and bleed brakes.
- **Cost:** $40–$120 per caliper

## 5. BRAKE MASTER CYLINDER REPLACEMENT

### Symptoms of Failed Master Cylinder
- Brake pedal goes to the floor (no resistance)
- Brake fluid in the cylinder is dark/contaminated
- Brake fluid leaks from the back of the cylinder into the engine bay (internal seal failure)
- Soft pedal that does not improve after bleeding
- Uneven braking between front and rear circuits

### Procedure

1. Disconnect battery negative terminal.
2. Remove the brake fluid reservoir from the top of the master cylinder (held by clips or bolts, clips usually).
3. Disconnect all brake lines from the master cylinder (4 fittings, 10–12mm) — have a catch pan ready; brake fluid will spill.
4. Locate the master cylinder pushrod connection inside the car at the top of the brake pedal (under the dashboard). It is connected by a clevis pin (9mm).
5. Remove the clevis pin — the pushrod will separate from the pedal.
6. Remove the 2–4 mounting bolts securing the master cylinder to the firewall/booster (12mm, inside engine bay).
7. Withdraw the master cylinder and any rubber seals/gasket from the firewall opening.
8. Install a new master cylinder (OEM recommended to ensure correct bore size and internal passages).
9. Torque mounting bolts to **14 Nm**.
10. Reconnect the pushrod with a new clevis pin.
11. Reconnect all 4 brake lines and torque to **11 Nm** (do not over-tighten — soft copper can crack).
12. Fill the brake fluid reservoir with DOT 3 or DOT 4 fluid (do not mix types).
13. Bleed the entire brake system (see section 6).

**Master Cylinder Part:** OEM $80–$200; Remanufactured $40–$100

## 6. BRAKE FLUID FLUSH & BLEEDING SEQUENCE

### When Needed
- Every 2 years or 40,000 km (brake fluid absorbs moisture and degrades)
- After any brake system work (pad/rotor/caliper replacement)
- If brake fluid is dark brown or black (indicates contamination)
- If brake pedal feels spongy (air in lines)

### Brake Fluid Specification
- **Type:** DOT 3 or DOT 4 (check door jamb sticker or owner's manual)
- **Do NOT mix DOT 3 and DOT 4** — they have different viscosity characteristics
- **Do NOT use DOT 5 (silicone-based)** — not compatible with Yaris seals

### Bleeding Sequence
Brakes must be bled in a specific order to ensure all air bubbles are removed:
1. **Right Rear (RR)**
2. **Left Rear (LR)**
3. **Right Front (RF)**
4. **Left Front (LF)**

This sequence ensures that air trapped in the rear circuits is pushed out first, then the front circuits are bled last (which are typically closest to the master cylinder and need less aggressive bleeding).

### Tools Required
- Brake bleeder kit (handheld vacuum bleeder or pressurized bleeder — easiest)
- OR: 2-person manual bleeding method (one person pumps pedal, second opens bleeder screws)
- 8–9mm open-end wrench (to fit around bleeder screws)
- Catch container for old brake fluid
- Fresh brake fluid (1–2 liters)

### Brake Bleeding Procedure (Handheld Vacuum Bleeder Method — Easiest)

1. **Preparation:**
   - Park on level ground
   - Turn engine OFF (do NOT start during bleeding — risks introducing air)
   - Fill the master cylinder reservoir to MAX with fresh brake fluid

2. **Bleed Right Rear (RR) wheel:**
   - Raise rear right of vehicle on jack stands
   - Locate the RR wheel bleeder screw (small bolt on the wheel cylinder or caliper, typically facing upward or to the side)
   - Attach the vacuum bleeder hose to the bleeder screw
   - Loosen the bleeder screw 1/2 turn
   - Pump the vacuum bleeder handle (pulls vacuum) until fresh fluid appears with no air bubbles (typically 5–10 strokes)
   - Close the bleeder screw
   - Remove the hose and move to the next wheel

3. **Bleed Left Rear (LR) wheel:**
   - Repeat the above procedure at the LR bleeder screw

4. **Bleed Right Front (RF) wheel:**
   - Lower the rear and raise the front right on jack stands
   - Repeat the vacuum bleeding procedure at the RF wheel (caliper bleeder screw)

5. **Bleed Left Front (LF) wheel:**
   - Raise the front left on jack stands
   - Repeat the vacuum bleeding procedure at the LF wheel

6. **Final Checks:**
   - Check brake fluid level in master cylinder — top up with fresh fluid if needed
   - Pump the brake pedal 5 times with the car raised — it should feel firm and not sink
   - If pedal feels soft: repeat the entire bleeding sequence

### Manual Bleeding Procedure (2-Person Method)

1. With the car parked and engine off, fill the master cylinder reservoir.
2. Person 1 sits in the driver's seat.
3. Person 2 stands beside the wheel being bled.
4. Person 2 locates the bleeder screw and attaches a clear hose to it (other end goes into a catch container).
5. Person 1 pumps the brake pedal 5 times steadily, then holds pressure on the pedal.
6. Person 2 opens the bleeder screw 1/2 turn — air and old fluid will flow into the container.
7. When the flow stops (brake pedal pressure drops), Person 2 closes the bleeder screw.
8. Person 1 releases the pedal, and the cycle repeats.
9. Continue until fresh, air-free fluid flows (typically 8–15 cycles per wheel).
10. Move to the next wheel in sequence (RR → LR → RF → LF) and repeat.

### Bleeder Screw Stuck?
If a bleeder screw is seized:
1. Do NOT force it — it can snap off.
2. Soak with penetrant oil (WD-40, Liquid Wrench) for 15 minutes.
3. Tap gently with a small hammer to break the corrosion.
4. Try opening with a 6-point wrench (not 12-point) for better grip.
5. If still stuck: take to a shop with pneumatic tools.

## 7. ABS WHEEL SPEED SENSOR REPLACEMENT

### Location
- Each wheel has one wheel speed sensor — 4 total (RR, LR, RF, LF)
- Sensors are mounted on the steering knuckle (front) or axle housing (rear)
- A reluctor ring (metal ring with teeth) on the hub or CV axle passes by the sensor as it rotates

### Symptoms of Failed Sensor
- ABS light on
- ABS disabled (no anti-lock braking available)
- Possible speedometer errors

### Procedure (Front Example)

1. Raise and support vehicle on jack stands.
2. Remove wheel.
3. Locate the wheel speed sensor on the steering knuckle (bolted to the knuckle, sensor tip pointing toward the reluctor ring/tone wheel on the hub).
4. Disconnect the sensor wiring connector (push-tab type).
5. Remove the single 10mm bolt securing the sensor to the knuckle.
6. Withdraw the sensor straight out.
7. Install new sensor and torque bolt to **8 Nm** (not too tight — tightness can affect sensor gap).
8. Reconnect the wiring connector.
9. Clear ABS codes with a scanner.
10. Test drive to verify ABS warning light is off.

**Wheel Speed Sensor Part:** $25–$60 each

## 8. PARKING BRAKE ADJUSTMENT

### Parking Brake Type
The XP90 Yaris uses a **cable-actuated drum brake** for the parking brake. The rear drum brakes serve double duty as service brakes and parking brake.

### When to Adjust
- Parking brake handle requires excessive travel (>6 clicks) to hold the car on an incline
- Parking brake doesn't hold vehicle on a slope
- Parking brake is stuck in the engaged position

### Adjustment Procedure

1. Engage the parking brake fully (pull handle all the way back).
2. Raise the rear of the vehicle on jack stands.
3. Spin each rear wheel — they should NOT spin (locked by the brake).
4. If wheels spin: brakes need adjustment (usually because rear drums are worn).
5. Locate the parking brake cable adjustment point — typically under the car near the rear axle, where the cable passes through an adjuster (metal hex nut or clamp).
6. With a wrench, tighten the cable adjuster (rotate clockwise) approximately 1/2 turn.
7. Spin the rear wheels again — they should now be locked.
8. Release the parking brake and spin wheels — they should spin freely.
9. If adjustment is correct: tighten the cable adjuster lock nut to prevent loosening.
10. Test on a slope to verify the parking brake holds the vehicle.

> Note: If the parking brake handle still requires >6 clicks after cable adjustment, the rear brake drums may be worn excessively. Shoe and drum service may be needed (see section 3).
