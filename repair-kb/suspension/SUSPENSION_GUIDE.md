# Suspension Guide — 2011 Toyota Yaris XP90

---

## 1. FRONT MACPHERSON STRUT REPLACEMENT

### Symptoms of Worn Struts
- Bouncy ride / car bounces over bumps
- Excessive body lean in cornering
- Soft suspension (car sags on one side)
- Knocking noise from suspension on bumps
- Fluid leaking from strut body

### Tools Required
- Spring compressor tool (critical safety tool) — rent from auto parts store
- Strut removal tool or heavy-duty socket set
- 14mm, 17mm, 19mm sockets
- Torque wrench
- Alignment machine (for post-replacement wheel alignment)

### Parts Required
- Front strut assembly (Toyota Part: 48530-XX150 — varies by side/market) — OEM $100–$200 each; Aftermarket $40–$100
- Strut bearing/mount kit (includes: strut upper mount, bearing, spring seat)
- New brake hose bracket (optional, if worn)
- Wheel alignment (necessary after replacement)

### Procedure

#### Preparation
1. Loosen wheel lug nuts slightly with car on ground.
2. Raise and support vehicle on jack stands.
3. Remove wheel.
4. Disconnect the brake hose bracket from the strut (1 bolt, 10mm).
5. Disconnect ABS wheel speed sensor connector (if equipped) — 1 bolt, and push-tab connector.

#### Strut Removal
1. **VERY IMPORTANT:** Before removing the strut, the spring must be compressed using a spring compressor tool.
2. Install spring compressor tool on the coil spring:
   - Position compressor above the spring's midpoint
   - Tighten clockwise until spring is compressed approximately 1 inch (25mm)
   - Verify spring is fully compressed — the spring should not be fully extended within its towers
3. Remove the 2 bolts connecting the strut lower arm to the steering knuckle (17mm bolts, typically 167 Nm torque). These are the "pinch bolts" at the back of the knuckle.
4. Remove the 3 bolts securing the strut upper mount to the body (inside engine bay, behind the strut tower) — 12–14mm bolts, typically 35 Nm.
5. Carefully pull the strut assembly downward and out of the vehicle.

#### Strut Disassembly (to remove old spring)
1. Place strut in a strut vise or soft-jaw vise (do not clamp the body tightly — it can dent).
2. **Keep the spring compressor installed and compressed.**
3. Using a large socket that fits over the piston rod top, hold the rod in place while unthreading the piston rod nut (17–19mm) with a wrench. The nut is typically tight.
4. Once nut is removed, the spring retainer, bearing, and upper mount can be lifted off.
5. **Very slowly and carefully** release the spring compressor tool. The spring will expand. Keep it compressed until it is free of the lower seat.
6. Remove the old spring from the compressor.

#### Inspection and Reassembly
1. Inspect the new strut rod for smoothness by hand — pull the rod in and out gently. Resistance should be smooth throughout.
2. If rod is stiff or sticky: soak the rod in penetrant oil for 15 minutes and work it back and forth.
3. Prepare new spring with compressor tool — compress approximately 1 inch.
4. Install lower spring seat on the strut body (metal cup) — ensure it sits flat.
5. Install compressed spring on the strut, ensuring spring coils engage properly in both seats.
6. Install the strut bearing / top mount assembly:
   - Bearing goes first (ball bearing allows steering rotation without binding)
   - Spring seat (usually metal plate)
   - Upper mount (rubber cushion that connects to body)
7. Install new piston rod nut and torque to 35–40 Nm while holding the rod steady with a socket.
8. **SLOWLY release the spring compressor** — monitor the spring as it expands to ensure it seats correctly in both upper and lower seats.
9. Remove strut from vise and spring compressor tool.

#### Installation
1. Align the strut assembly into the upper tower cavity from below.
2. Install the 3 upper mounting bolts (12–14mm) — torque to **35 Nm**.
3. Align the strut lower arm with the steering knuckle pinch bolts.
4. Install the 2 pinch bolts (17mm) — torque to **167 Nm**.
5. Reconnect ABS wheel speed sensor connector and bolt.
6. Reconnect brake hose bracket.
7. Reinstall wheel and torque lug nuts to **103 Nm**.
8. Lower vehicle to ground and bounce each corner a few times to settle the suspension.

#### Wheel Alignment
- **CRITICAL:** After strut replacement, front wheel alignment MUST be checked and adjusted.
- Take to an alignment shop — they will set camber, caster, and toe.
- Expected post-alignment specifications are listed in section 7 below.

## 2. FRONT LOWER CONTROL ARM & BUSHING REPLACEMENT

### Symptoms of Worn Control Arm
- Clunking noise when turning or hitting bumps
- Excessive play in steering wheel
- Uneven tire wear (pulling to one side)
- Vibration while braking

### Procedure

1. Raise and support vehicle on jack stands.
2. Remove wheel.
3. Disconnect the ball joint nut from the steering knuckle:
   - Loosen the nut (19mm) connecting the ball joint to the knuckle
   - Use a ball joint separator tool to safely separate the tapered joint (prevents tearing the rubber boot)
   - Remove nut and withdraw the ball joint stud from the knuckle
4. Remove the 2 bolts (10mm each) securing the control arm to the frame/chassis — these are at the rear of the control arm, near the firewall.
5. Remove the control arm assembly.

#### Bushing Replacement
If only the bushings are worn (common after 100k+ km):
1. Take the removed control arm to a machine shop equipped with a hydraulic press.
2. Press out old bushings and press in new ones (Toyota Part: 48655-XX050 or equivalent).
3. Cost: typically $30–$80 labor + parts.

#### Control Arm Installation
1. Insert new control arm into frame brackets.
2. Install and torque the 2 frame bolts to **103 Nm**.
3. Reconnect the ball joint stud to the steering knuckle.
4. Torque the ball joint nut to **49 Nm**.
5. If equipped with a cotter pin: insert a new cotter pin through the hole (do not reuse old pin).
6. Reinstall wheel, torque to 103 Nm.
7. Check wheel alignment.

**Control Arm Part:** Toyota Part: 48068-XX060 (varies by side/market) — OEM $60–$150; Aftermarket $25–$80

## 3. REAR TORSION BEAM SUSPENSION — OVERVIEW & SHOCK REPLACEMENT

### Suspension Design
The rear of the XP90 Yaris uses a **torsion beam (or "coupled torsion axle")** design — not independent double-wishbone or multi-link. The beam is a single rigid tube that connects both wheel hubs and twists to absorb bumps from either wheel. This design:
- Is inexpensive to manufacture
- Provides good lateral stiffness for cornering
- Has a smooth, predictable ride
- Limits brake dive compared to independent setups

### What Can Fail on the Rear
1. **Rear shocks** (struts) — most common wear item
2. **Wheel bearings** — can wear, causing noise or play
3. **Torsion beam bushings** — rubber bushings wear, causing clunking
4. **Brake lines/hoses** — routed along the beam, can chafe
5. **Rear brake cylinders** — drum brakes on Yaris, cylinders can leak
6. **Stabilizer (anti-roll) bar links** — wear and clunk

### Rear Shock Replacement

Rear shocks on the XP90 Yaris are **bolt-on only** — no springs to compress (springs are separate coils or leafs not common on Yaris, or the torsion beam provides the spring).

#### Procedure
1. Raise and support vehicle safely on jack stands.
2. Remove rear wheel (if required for access — some vehicles allow shock replacement without wheel removal).
3. Locate rear shock (upper and lower attachment points):
   - **Upper mounting:** Inside the car, accessible from the rear parcel tray or cargo area. Remove trim panel to access the top of the shock.
   - **Lower mounting:** Bolted to the torsion beam axle housing.
4. Remove upper bolt first (typically 14mm, from inside car):
   - Remove rear parcel tray side trim or access panel
   - Remove 1 bolt connecting the shock rod to the body — torque spec **39 Nm** (removed)
5. Remove lower bolt (typically 14mm or 17mm) connecting shock to torsion beam:
   - Torque spec **74 Nm** (removed)
6. Withdraw shock downward and out.
7. Install new shock — align rod through upper tower hole.
8. Install upper bolt and torque to **39 Nm**.
9. Torque lower bolt to **74 Nm**.
10. Reinstall trim panels.
11. Reinstall wheel if removed.

**Rear Shock Part:** Toyota Part: 48530-XX252 (varies by side/market) — OEM $80–$150 each; Aftermarket $30–$60 each

## 4. FRONT WHEEL BEARING REPLACEMENT

### Symptoms of Worn Front Wheel Bearing
- Grinding noise from one wheel, especially when turning
- Wheel wobbles / excessive play on the hub
- Uneven tire wear on one wheel
- Vibration in steering wheel or pedals

### Bearing Type
Front wheel bearings on the XP90 are **pressed cartridge bearings** mounted on the steering knuckle. The bearing is a sealed unit and cannot be disassembled — entire bearing cartridge must be replaced.

### Tools Required
- Wheel bearing removal/installation press OR bearing puller kit
- Slide hammer (heavy puller)
- 17mm socket for axle nut
- Torque wrench

### Procedure

1. Raise and support vehicle on jack stands.
2. Remove wheel.
3. Remove brake caliper (see BRAKE_GUIDE.md) — do NOT let it hang by the brake line. Hang on a wire hook.
4. Remove brake rotor (2–3 screws behind rotor or rotor pulls off directly).
5. Remove the axle nut (17mm, very tight — typically 216 Nm installed) using an impact wrench or breaker bar.
6. Disconnect the steering tie rod end from the knuckle (see section 5 below for tie rod procedure).
7. Remove the 2 ball joint pinch bolts holding the control arm to the knuckle (see section 2 above).
8. The steering knuckle is now free — pull it straight outward off the axle stub.

#### Bearing Removal
The bearing is pressed into the knuckle bore. You will need either:
- A proper bearing press and tooling (take to a machine shop — typically $20–$50 labor)
- OR an impact-style bearing puller

Using a **bearing puller:**
1. Position the puller jaws inside the bearing race (the outer rim).
2. Install the center bolt through the puller and thread it into the wheel hub center hole (where the axle was).
3. Tighten the center bolt to draw the bearing straight out. It should come out with considerable force.
4. Once bearing is removed, clean the knuckle bore thoroughly.

#### Bearing Installation
1. Press new bearing into the knuckle bore — the bearing must seat fully and squarely.
2. Insert bearing from the hub side if using a press (or outside of knuckle if using puller method in reverse).
3. The bearing outer race should sit flush in the bore.

#### Knuckle Reassembly
1. Clean the axle spline thoroughly.
2. Slide the knuckle back onto the axle.
3. Reconnect the ball joint to the knuckle — torque pinch bolts to **147 Nm** (not 49 Nm like a separate ball joint — this is for the strut-to-knuckle pinch joint).
4. Reconnect the tie rod end — torque to **49 Nm**.
5. Install the new axle nut and torque to **216 Nm** — this is a very high torque and critical for bearing preload.
6. Reinstall rotor and brake caliper.
7. Reinstall wheel and torque lug nuts to **103 Nm**.
8. Check wheel alignment after any suspension work.

**Front Wheel Bearing Part:** Toyota Part: 90363-XX150 (varies by side/market) — OEM $40–$100; Aftermarket $20–$60

## 5. REAR WHEEL BEARING REPLACEMENT

Rear wheel bearings on the XP90 are similar to front bearings — **pressed cartridges**. However, the rear is a torsion beam axle, so the procedure differs slightly.

### Procedure

1. Raise and support vehicle on jack stands.
2. Remove rear wheel.
3. Remove brake drum (or rotor if disc brake equipped — rare on XP90 rear).
4. If equipped with ABS, disconnect the wheel speed sensor.
5. Remove the rear axle nut (same 216 Nm nut as front).
6. The rear hub/bearing assembly may be pressed onto the axle shaft. Use a bearing puller or take to a shop with a press.
7. Once bearing is out, install new bearing — should press straight on.
8. Reinstall axle nut and torque to **216 Nm**.
9. Reinstall brake drum/rotor.
10. Reinstall wheel.

**Rear Wheel Bearing Part:** Toyota Part: 90363-XX151 (varies, usually different part number than front) — $20–$60

## 6. TIE ROD END REPLACEMENT & TOE ADJUSTMENT

### Symptoms of Worn Tie Rod Ends
- Clunking in steering when going over bumps
- Excessive play in steering wheel (more than 1 inch / 2.5cm of play before wheels move)
- Uneven or feathered tire wear (tread worn at an angle)
- Steering wanders or drifts to one side

### Procedure

1. Raise and support vehicle on jack stands.
2. Remove wheel (optional but recommended).
3. Locate the tie rod end (connects the steering rack to the steering knuckle, near the brake rotor).
4. Loosen the jam nut on the tie rod (21–22mm, wrench only — do not remove).
5. Remove the cotter pin (if present) and unscrew the tie rod end nut (19mm) from the knuckle studs.
6. Use a ball joint separator tool to pop the tapered stud free from the knuckle (prevents tearing the rubber boot).
7. Unscrew the tie rod end from the tie rod thread — note how many turns it takes (this helps with reinstallation length).
8. Screw new tie rod end onto the threaded rod — thread in the same number of turns as the old one came out.
9. Reconnect the stud to the knuckle and torque the nut to **49 Nm**.
10. Install a new cotter pin.
11. Tighten the jam nut (21–22mm) to lock the tie rod end position — torque to approximately **50 Nm**.

### Wheel Alignment & Toe Adjustment

**Toe** is the angle of the wheels relative to the centerline of the vehicle. Toe adjustment is critical after tie rod work.

#### Toe Specification (2011 Yaris XP90)
- **Front toe (per side):** 0° to +0°20' (0 to 0.33 degrees per side; total toe is 0° to +0°40')
- **Rear toe:** Typically 0° to +0°20' per side (if adjustable; many Yaris models have fixed rear toe)

#### Checking Toe (DIY method — approximate)
1. Drive the vehicle straight on a flat surface.
2. Measure the distance between the front edge of both wheels at the centerline (front measurement).
3. Measure the distance between the rear edge of both wheels at the same height (rear measurement).
4. If the front measurement is equal to or slightly larger than the rear measurement: toe is correct (toe-in).
5. If front measurement is noticeably smaller: toe is too much (over-toe-in) — requires tie rod shortening.
6. If front is larger: toe is out — requires tie rod lengthening.

#### Professional Alignment
For precise toe adjustment, visit an alignment shop. Cost is typically $100–$200 for a 2-wheel alignment.

**Tie Rod End Part:** Toyota Part: 45046-XX050 (varies by side/market) — OEM $20–$60; Aftermarket $10–$30

## 7. WHEEL ALIGNMENT SPECIFICATIONS

### Front Alignment
| Parameter | Specification | Tolerance |
|-----------|---------------|-----------|
| Camber | -1°30' to +0°30' | ±0°30' |
| Caster | +2°45' to +4°15' | ±0°30' |
| Toe | 0° to +0°20' per side | ±0°10' |
| Total Toe | 0° to +0°40' | ±0°20' |

### Rear Alignment
| Parameter | Specification | Notes |
|-----------|---------------|-------|
| Toe | 0° to +0°20' per side | May be non-adjustable on torsion beam design |
| Camber | Not usually adjustable | Fixed by torsion beam design |

### When to Align
- After strut replacement
- After control arm replacement
- After tie rod replacement
- If vehicle pulls to one side during straight-line driving
- If tires are wearing unevenly

## 8. STEERING WHEEL REMOVAL & CLOCKSPRING REPLACEMENT

### Symptoms of Failed Clockspring
- Airbag warning light (SRS) constantly on
- Horn does not work
- Airbag warning light comes on and off intermittently
- Steering wheel tight or binding during rotation (rare)

### Clockspring Function
The clockspring (spiral cable) is a coil of electrical contacts that allows electrical signals (horn, airbag, radio controls) to pass from the steering column to the steering wheel as the wheel turns. The coil winds and unwinds as the steering rotates.

### Tools Required
- T20 Torx screwdriver (airbag module bolts are Torx)
- Various screwdrivers (trim removal)
- Steering wheel puller (optional; sometimes just lifts off)
- Socket set

### Procedure

#### **CRITICAL: Disable the SRS System**
**Before starting any work on the steering wheel or column, you MUST disable the airbag system to prevent accidental deployment.**

1. Turn off ignition.
2. **Disconnect the battery negative terminal and wait at least 90 seconds.** This discharges the airbag capacitor.
3. Do not apply power or crank the engine during this work.

#### Steering Wheel Removal
1. With power disconnected, locate the airbag module on the back of the steering wheel (center of the wheel, large plastic shroud).
2. Remove the 2 Torx bolts (T20, approximately 6–8 Nm) securing the airbag module to the wheel.
3. Carefully lift the airbag module away — it is connected by 2 wires (connector clips). Disconnect the airbag squib connectors (push-tab type).
4. Set the airbag module aside on a flat surface, **cushion facing up** — never point it at your face.
5. Remove the horn switch connector (push-clip).
6. Remove the steering wheel hub bolt (17–19mm, center of wheel) — torque spec **36 Nm** (removed).
7. Install a steering wheel puller (3-jaw type) on the wheel hub, or simply rock the wheel gently side-to-side while pulling outward (most wheels will pull free with modest force).
8. Once wheel is free, mark the column position relative to the wheel with a marker or tape to allow correct reinstallation alignment.

#### Clockspring Removal
1. Remove the upper and lower steering column covers (held by screws or clips).
2. Disconnect the clockspring connectors (usually 2 connectors: one for horn/controls, one for airbag signal) — push-tab.
3. Remove the 3 clockspring mounting bolts (10mm) to the column.
4. **Before fully removing the old clockspring:** If the steering wheel has been rotated more than 2–3 full turns with the clockspring disconnected, the coil may be over-wound and damaged. Note the current steering wheel position.
5. Carefully withdraw the clockspring upward and away from the column.

#### Clockspring Installation
1. **Critical step:** The clockspring must be centered before installation.
2. With the new clockspring out of the box, verify that the wheel indicator mark is at the 12 o'clock position (straight/center position).
3. Install the new clockspring onto the column — align the mounting holes.
4. Install the 3 mounting bolts and torque to approximately 8 Nm (snug, not tight).
5. Connect the clockspring connectors — ensure they click fully.
6. Reinstall steering column covers.

#### Steering Wheel Reinstallation
1. Connect the horn switch connector.
2. Align the steering wheel's previous mark with the column's 12 o'clock position (straight ahead).
3. Slide wheel onto hub and install the center bolt — torque to **36 Nm**.
4. Connect the airbag squib connectors — they must click fully.
5. Install the airbag module onto the back of the wheel — install Torx bolts and torque to **6 Nm**.
6. **Do NOT skip this step:** Reconnect the battery negative terminal.
7. Turn on ignition and verify the airbag light turns on momentarily, then off.
8. Test the horn — should work immediately.
9. Test that the airbag warning light is off.

**Clockspring Part:** Toyota Part: 84306-XX030 (varies by market) — OEM $50–$150; Aftermarket $20–$80
