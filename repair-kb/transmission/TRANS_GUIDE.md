# Transmission Guide — 2011 Toyota Yaris XP90

---

## 1. MANUAL TRANSMISSION — C50 / C52

### Transmission Specifications
- **Type:** C50 (5-speed) or C52 (5-speed, varies by market)
- **Fluid:** 75W-90 Gear Oil (not engine oil)
- **Capacity:** Approximately 2.8–3.2 liters (fill plug to level)
- **No synchro mesh issues:** Toyota manual boxes are known for reliability

### Fluid Change Procedure

1. Warm the transmission by driving the vehicle.
2. Raise and support the vehicle on jack stands.
3. Locate the transmission drain plug (bottom-center of the transmission case, 14mm bolt) and place a drain pan underneath.
4. Remove the drain plug and allow the old fluid to drain completely (5–10 minutes).
5. Wipe the drain plug clean and inspect the magnet attached to it:
   - Light dust is normal
   - Heavy metal particles indicate internal wear (consider transmission inspection)
   - Chips or large fragments suggest bearing/gear damage
6. Reinstall the drain plug and torque to **27 Nm**.
7. Locate the transmission fill plug (side of the transmission case, same height as the drain plug, 14mm bolt).
8. Remove the fill plug.
9. Pour in fresh 75W-90 gear oil:
   - Start with 2.8 liters
   - Continue until fluid level reaches the bottom of the fill plug hole (fluid should drip out of the hole when full)
   - Use a siphon pump or funnel with a long tube to reach into the fill hole
10. Reinstall the fill plug and torque to **27 Nm**.
11. Check fluid level (engine off, level ground):
    - Start the engine, shift through all gears, return to Neutral
    - Top up the fill plug if the level has dropped
12. Road test to verify smooth shifts.

**Fluid (75W-90 Gear Oil):** $20–$40 per liter; 3L bottle $50–$100

### Clutch System Overview

**No Adjustment Required:** The Toyota Yaris manual transmission uses a **hydraulic clutch** (not cable-operated). The system is self-adjusting — no pedal free-play adjustment is needed.

#### Hydraulic Clutch Components
1. **Master cylinder:** Mounted under the dash above the pedal, connected to the clutch pedal arm
2. **Clutch line:** Runs from the master cylinder to the slave cylinder (under the car at the transmission)
3. **Slave cylinder:** Mounted on the transmission bellhousing, pushes the clutch release fork
4. **Release fork:** Pivots inside the transmission bell, pushed by the slave piston to disengage the clutch disc

#### Symptoms of Clutch Wear
- Clutch pedal goes almost to the floor before engagement
- Severe slipping under acceleration (RPM climbs but car doesn't speed up)
- Burning smell from the clutch (overheating due to slip)
- Chattering or shuddering when engaging from a stop

### Clutch Replacement Overview

Clutch replacement requires:
1. **Transmission removal** (engine remains in car; transmission is lowered from the engine)
2. **Clutch disc replacement** (friction surface wears over time — typically 150k–200k km lifespan)
3. **Pressure plate replacement** (spring tension weakens with age)
4. **Release bearing replacement** (worn at same time as clutch)
5. **Transmission reinstallation** and fluid refill

**Typical labor cost:** $400–$800 (highly variable by shop and location)
**Parts cost:** OEM clutch kit (disc + pressure plate + release bearing) $100–$250

#### DIY Feasibility
Clutch replacement is **challenging for DIY** due to:
- Transmission removal (requires jack, engine support, alignment)
- Flywheel access (need to support the transmission as it's removed)
- Pedal adjustment after reinstallation (master cylinder rod length, free-play)
- Recommended for experienced mechanics or a professional shop

## 2. AUTOMATIC TRANSMISSION — U340E

### Transmission Specifications
- **Type:** U340E (4-speed automatic, Toyota/Aisin)
- **Fluid:** Toyota WS ATF (World Standard Automatic Transmission Fluid)
- **Capacity:** Approximately 3.7 liters (drain and refill), 5.5–6.0 liters (full system with converter)
- **Shift points:** Computer-controlled via ECM based on throttle position, vehicle speed, and load
- **Torque converter:** Fluid-coupled coupling (no direct mechanical connection), provides smooth starts

### Transmission Fluid Change

**Drain-and-Refill Method (Partial):**

1. Warm the transmission (drive the vehicle for 5–10 minutes).
2. Raise and support the vehicle on jack stands.
3. Locate the transmission drain plug (bottom-center of the pan, typically 10–12mm bolt).
4. Place a drain pan underneath (ATF will spill).
5. Remove the drain plug and allow the old fluid to drain (5–10 minutes).
6. **Optional:** Remove the transmission pan (6–8 bolts, 10mm):
   - Loosen all bolts halfway first, then fully remove every other bolt
   - This prevents the pan from tipping and spilling the remaining fluid
   - Once the pan is partially loose, loosen the remaining bolts and carefully lower the pan
   - Drain any fluid still in the pan into the drain pan
   - Inspect the pan magnet (light particles are normal; large chips indicate transmission wear)
   - If the pan is very dirty, flush with transmission fluid and a lint-free cloth
7. If the pan was removed, reinstall it with new gasket and torque bolts to **10 Nm** in a star pattern.
8. Reinstall the drain plug (if removed) and torque to **12 Nm**.
9. Lower the vehicle to the ground.
10. Add fresh Toyota WS ATF:
    - Pop the transmission dipstick (long yellow handle, located on the right side of the transmission under the hood)
    - Wipe the stick clean
    - Reinstall and pull it out again to check the level
    - If level is low, add ATF in 0.5 liter increments through the dipstick tube
    - Check level after each addition — do not overfill (risk of seal leakage)
11. Reinstall the dipstick.
12. Start the engine and allow it to idle for 2 minutes.
13. Shift through all positions (P → R → N → D) slowly, holding each for 3 seconds, then return to P.
14. Check the fluid level again (engine idling) — top up if needed.
15. Road test the vehicle:
    - Verify smooth shifts in all forward gears
    - Verify the transmission does not slip (RPM climbs excessively)
    - Verify no harsh bangs or clunks during shifts

**Note:** Drain-and-refill only replaces about 50% of the transmission fluid (the torque converter retains fluid). A complete fluid flush with a transmission fluid exchange machine ($100–$200) is recommended every 80,000–100,000 km for longer transmission life.

**Toyota WS ATF:** $15–$25 per liter; full flush 5–6 liters cost $75–$150

### Common Transmission Fault Codes (1KR-FE with U340E)

**P0730 — Incorrect Gear Ratio**
- Symptoms: Transmission won't shift properly or slips between gears
- Causes: Low ATF, worn clutch packs, faulty shift solenoid
- Fix: Check ATF level; change fluid; test solenoids; if codes persist, transmission overhaul needed

**P0705 — Transmission Range Sensor Circuit Malfunction**
- Symptoms: Transmission won't shift; reverse lights don't work; start inhibitor fails
- Causes: Faulty range switch, wiring fault, cable misadjustment
- Fix: Check neutral safety switch; test wiring; adjust shift cable if needed

**P0741 — Torque Converter Clutch Performance**
- Symptoms: Shudder at highway speeds; poor fuel economy at cruise
- Causes: Degraded ATF, failed TCC solenoid, worn converter
- Fix: Change ATF with fresh fluid; test solenoid resistance (spec 5.0–5.6 Ω); if persists, converter replacement needed

**P0760 — Shift Solenoid B**
**P0765 — Shift Solenoid C**
**P0770 — Shift Solenoid D**
- Symptoms: Transmission stuck in 3rd gear (limp mode)
- Causes: Failed solenoid, open/shorted wiring
- Fix: Disconnect solenoid and measure resistance (spec ~6–8 Ω depending on solenoid); test 12V signal from ECM; replace solenoid if failed

**Part — Shift Solenoid:** OEM $30–$100; Aftermarket $20–$60 each

### Limp Mode (Failsafe)
If the ECM detects a critical transmission fault (shift solenoid failure, TCC fault, range sensor issue), the transmission enters "Limp Mode":
- **Symptom:** Transmission locks in 3rd gear or neutral, vehicle is driveable but severely limited
- **Diagnosis:** Scan with OBD-II to find fault code
- **Fix:** Repair the specific fault code; clear codes; transmission should exit limp mode

## 3. CV AXLE (FRONT DRIVESHAFT) REPLACEMENT

### CV Axle Overview
The front axles are connected via Constant Velocity (CV) joints at both ends:
- **Inner CV joint:** Connects to the differential; has flexibility to absorb vertical suspension travel
- **Outer CV joint:** Connects to the wheel hub; allows full steering angle rotation while transferring power

### Symptoms of Failed CV Axle
- Clicking or popping noise when turning (outer CV joint wear — worn rollers)
- Vibration during acceleration (inner or outer joint loose)
- Torn CV boot with grease splatter (CV joint contaminated with water/dirt)
- Clunking under load (worn CV joint cage or rollers)

### Procedure

#### Driver's Side (Left Front)

1. Raise and support the vehicle on jack stands.
2. Remove the wheel.
3. Remove the ABS wheel speed sensor connector (if equipped) — 1 10mm bolt.
4. Remove the brake caliper and rotor (see BRAKE_GUIDE.md).
5. Remove the steering tie rod end nut (19mm) and separate from the knuckle using a ball joint tool.
6. Remove the lower ball joint nut (19mm) and separate the control arm from the knuckle.
7. The steering knuckle is now free — pull it outward and to the rear.
8. **Locate the inner CV joint at the differential:**
   - The axle enters the transmission/differential at the driver's side of the engine bay
   - The inner joint is held in place by internal circlip (snap ring) in the differential bore
9. To remove the axle with the inner joint:
   - Use a pry bar positioned between the differential case and the inner CV joint bracket
   - Tap the pry bar lightly with a hammer to push the axle outward
   - Once the inner CV joint pops out of the differential, pull the entire axle straight out
   - If the axle is stuck: have an assistant hold the steering knuckle while you apply force
10. Once the axle is free, you can remove the inner CV joint from the axle (slide hammer or pull from the other side), or replace the entire axle as an assembly.

#### Passenger's Side (Right Front)

1. Follow steps 1–7 above (same suspension disconnect procedure).
2. The passenger side axle typically exits the transmission at a different angle (right-hand side of the engine).
3. Remove the axle the same way — pry at the differential to pop the inner joint free, then pull the axle out.

#### Installation

1. **If replacing the entire axle:**
   - Slide the new axle into the differential from the side until the inner CV joint seals and locks into place (you will feel/hear a click as the circlip seats)
   - Reconnect the outer CV joint to the wheel hub (the outer joint splines into the hub center bore)
2. **If just replacing the boots or inner CV joint:**
   - Install new CV boot on the axle (before reconnecting to differential)
   - Press the inner CV joint onto the axle splines
   - Install new circlip on the axle shaft (if needed)
3. Reconnect steering knuckle to the control arm (torque ball joint nut to **49 Nm**).
4. Reconnect tie rod end (torque to **49 Nm**).
5. Reinstall brake rotor and caliper.
6. Reinstall wheel speed sensor.
7. Reinstall wheel and torque lug nuts to **103 Nm**.
8. Test drive to verify no clicking during turns.

**CV Axle Part (Complete assembly):** Toyota Part: 43410-XX060 (varies by side/market) — OEM $80–$150; Remanufactured $40–$80

## 4. MOTOR MOUNTS — INSPECTION & REPLACEMENT

### Motor Mount Function
Motor mounts insulate the engine/transmission from the chassis. They:
- Dampen vibration from the engine
- Limit engine movement during acceleration/deceleration
- Reduce noise and harshness transmitted to the cabin and chassis

### Mount Locations (Typical)
1. **Right engine mount:** Connects engine to the right side of the frame
2. **Left engine mount:** Connects transmission to the left side of the frame
3. **Rear transmission mount:** Connects transmission to the rear subframe
4. **Front/Support mount:** May be under the engine or between engine and frame

### Symptoms of Failed Motor Mount
- **Clunking** when shifting from Park to Drive or Reverse
- **Engine rocking** excessively when accelerating (visually obvious if you watch the engine with the hood open while someone accelerates)
- **Vibration** transmitted to the steering wheel or pedals (especially at idle)
- **Noise** during rough acceleration or deceleration

### Visual Inspection

1. With the engine running, have an assistant shift from P to D while you observe the engine under the hood.
2. The engine should rock slightly (normal is 0.5–1 inch).
3. Excessive rocking (>2 inches) or clunking indicates a failed mount.
4. Look at each visible motor mount rubber pad:
   - If the rubber is cracked, split, or missing: mount is failed
   - If the mount is compressed fully with the engine resting on the metal bracket: failed
5. A collapsed or missing mount rubber will cause the engine to sit lower on one side.

### Motor Mount Replacement

**Difficulty:** Medium — requires engine support jack and careful work to prevent dropping the engine.

**Typical procedure (Right side mount):**

1. Disconnect battery negative terminal.
2. Place an engine support jack under the engine oil pan (wood block under the jack to spread the load).
3. Raise the jack slightly to support the engine weight without lifting it fully off the frame.
4. Remove the bolt(s) connecting the mount to the engine/transmission.
5. Remove the bolt(s) connecting the mount to the frame.
6. Withdraw the old mount.
7. Install new motor mount and torque all bolts to specification (typically **52 Nm** engine side, **87 Nm** frame side — verify with manual).
8. Lower the engine support jack carefully.
9. Reconnect battery and test.

**Motor Mount Part:** OEM $25–$80 per mount; Aftermarket $15–$50

**Recommended approach:** Motor mounts should be replaced in pairs (left and right) to maintain balanced engine isolation.
